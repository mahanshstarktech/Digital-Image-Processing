01

Image Degradation & Restoration Model

🔥 90% · Part A/B/C

What is Image Degradation?

Image Degradation is the process where the quality of a digital image is reduced due to some degradation function (like motion blur, atmospheric distortion) and/or additive noise. The degraded image g(x,y) is what we actually observe. The goal of Image Restoration is to recover the original image f(x,y) from the degraded g(x,y).

Key difference from Enhancement:

  • Enhancement (Unit 3): Subjective improvement — no mathematical model; just make it "look better".
  • Restoration (Unit 4): Objective, model-based — we know the degradation process and try to mathematically reverse it.

The Degradation Model — Most Important Diagram

Fig 1.1 — Image Degradation & Restoration Model — Draw this in EVERY Part C question!
Degradation Model — Spatial Domain
g(x,y) = h(x,y) ★ f(x,y) + η(x,y)
★ = convolution, h(x,y) = degradation/PSF, η = additive noise
Degradation Model — Frequency Domain
G(u,v) = H(u,v) · F(u,v) + N(u,v)
Convolution in spatial = multiplication in frequency domain (from Unit 3 DFT property!)
🔗
Connection to Unit 3 (Fourier Transform)

The shift from h★f (convolution) to H·F (multiplication) directly uses the Convolution Theorem from Unit 3: DFT(h★f) = DFT(h)·DFT(f). This is the entire reason we work in frequency domain for restoration — division is much simpler than deconvolution. Always mention this connection in Part C answers.

Types of Degradation

  • Motion blur: Camera or object moves during exposure → uniform smearing
  • Out-of-focus blur (defocus): Lens focused incorrectly → disc-shaped PSF
  • Atmospheric turbulence: Light passing through turbulent air → random blur
  • Additive noise: Electronic sensors, transmission errors → random pixel perturbations

Image Degradation & Restoration Model:

Degradation Process: An original image f(x,y) passes through a degradation function H and additive noise η(x,y) is introduced, producing the degraded image:
g(x,y) = h(x,y) ★ f(x,y) + η(x,y)
In frequency domain: G(u,v) = H(u,v)·F(u,v) + N(u,v)

Noise Models — All 6 PDFs

🔥 90% · Part C Must-Know

What is Noise in Images?

Noise is any unwanted perturbation to the pixel values. It originates from image acquisition (sensor noise, thermal noise), transmission errors, or quantization. Noise is modeled as a random variable with a specific probability density function (PDF).

Most noise is additive: g(x,y) = f(x,y) + η(x,y), where η is the noise term.

Why "additive"? Because in CCD/CMOS sensors, thermal electron fluctuations simply add to the signal. It can be processed and subtracted. Multiplicative noise (like speckle in SAR) is harder to handle.

The 6 Noise Models — Formulas & Shapes

1. Gaussian Noise

p(z) = (1/√(2πσ)) · e^(-(z-μ)²/(2σ²))

μ = mean intensity, σ² = variance (spread)
Shape: Bell curve, symmetric about μ
Cause: Electronic sensor noise, poor illumination, thermal noise
Use: Most commonly assumed noise model (easiest math)

⭐ Most important noise type — appears in ALL years. Symmetric PDF = equal probability of positive and negative perturbations.

2. Rayleigh Noise

p(z) = (2(z-a)/b)·e^(-(z-a)²/b), z≥a
p(z) = 0, z<a

Mean: a + √(πb/4)
Variance: b(4-π)/4
Shape: Right-skewed, peak at z = a + √(b/2)
Cause: Range imaging (radar), distance measurements
Note: Not symmetric — skewed to the right

3. Erlang (Gamma) Noise

p(z) = (aᵇz^(b-1)/(b-1)!)·e^(-az), z≥0
p(z) = 0, z<0

Mean: b/a, Variance: b/a²
Shape: Right-skewed, can vary from exponential to near-Gaussian
Cause: Laser imaging, positron emission tomography
Note: When b=1, becomes Exponential noise

4. Exponential Noise

p(z) = a·e^(-az), z≥0
p(z) = 0, z<0

Mean: 1/a, Variance: 1/a²
Shape: Monotonically decreasing from z=0
Cause: Special case of Erlang (b=1)
Note: Steepest descent from zero

5. Uniform Noise

p(z) = 1/(b-a), a≤z≤b
p(z) = 0, otherwise

Mean: (a+b)/2, Variance: (b-a)²/12
Shape: Flat rectangle between a and b
Cause: Quantization noise (least-significant-bit rounding)
Use: Theoretical analysis, simulation

6. Salt & Pepper (Impulse) Noise

p(z) = Pa if z=a (pepper)
p(z) = Pb if z=b (salt)
p(z) = 0 otherwise

a = dark value (0), b = bright value (255)
Shape: Two impulses at opposite extremes
Cause: Faulty pixels, transmission errors, bit errors
Removal: Median filter (BEST for this noise!)

⭐ S&P noise → ALWAYS use median filter. Mean filters SMEAR the noise. Median removes isolated extreme values perfectly.

Gaussian → Rayleigh → Erlang → Uniform → U... wait — use "GRE UES":
Gaussian, Rayleigh, Erlang, Uniform, Exponential, Salt-&-Pepper

Estimating Noise Parameters from Histogram

In practice, we estimate noise type by analyzing the image histogram in a uniform region (where all pixels should ideally have the same value):

  • If histogram looks like a bell curve → Gaussian noise
  • If histogram is flat → Uniform noise
  • If histogram has two isolated spikes at extremes → Salt & Pepper
  • If histogram is right-skewed → Rayleigh or Erlang

Q. Discuss different types of noise probability density functions in detail. Explain the image restoration process in the presence of noise only. Give example of at least one linear and one non-linear filter that can be used for the restoration.

Noise Probability Density Functions in Images:

Noise is a random perturbation to pixel values. Its statistical behavior is described by its PDF p(z), where z is the gray level. The shape of the PDF determines which filters are most effective.

1. Gaussian Noise:
p(z) = (1/√(2πσ)) · e^(-(z-μ)²/(2σ²))
Mean = μ, Variance = σ². Symmetric bell-shaped PDF. Most common in electronic systems. Good for linear filter removal.

2. Rayleigh Noise:
p(z) = (2(z-a)/b)·e^(-(z-a)²/b) for z ≥ a; 0 otherwise
Right-skewed. Common in range imaging. Mean = a + √(πb/4).

3. Erlang (Gamma) Noise:
p(z) = (aᵇ·z^(b-1)/(b-1)!)·e^(-az) for z ≥ 0; 0 otherwise
Also right-skewed. Used in laser imaging. When b=1, gives Exponential noise.

4. Uniform Noise:
p(z) = 1/(b-a) for a ≤ z ≤ b; 0 otherwise
Flat PDF. Caused by quantization. Symmetric.

5. Salt & Pepper (Impulse) Noise:
p(z) = Pa at z=a (dark) and Pb at z=b (bright); 0 otherwise
Random black/white pixels. Caused by bit errors, faulty sensors.

[Draw all 5 PDFs as simple plots — bell curve, right-skewed, flat rectangle, two spikes]

Image Restoration in Presence of Noise Only (H = identity):
When no blur, degradation model simplifies to: g(x,y) = f(x,y) + η(x,y)
Goal: estimate f(x,y) from g(x,y) by filtering out η.

Linear Filter Example — Arithmetic Mean Filter:
f̂(x,y) = (1/mn) · Σ g(s,t) over neighborhood Sxy
Reduces Gaussian noise. Smooth uniform regions but blurs edges. Simple to implement.

Non-Linear Filter Example — Median Filter:
f̂(x,y) = median{g(s,t) | (s,t) ∈ Sxy}
Best for salt & pepper noise. Sort neighborhood values, pick middle. Preserves edges better than arithmetic mean. Non-linear because output ≠ linear combination of inputs.

June 2023 · Part A Q8 · 2 marks | Also Part C Q2 sub-question
Erlang (Gamma) Noise:
PDF: p(z) = (aᵇ·z^(b-1)/(b-1)!) · e^(-az), z ≥ 0
Parameters: a > 0 (scale), b = positive integer (shape). Right-skewed distribution. Mean = b/a, Variance = b/a². Used to model noise in laser illumination systems and in positron emission tomography. When b=1, the Erlang distribution reduces to the Exponential distribution.

Impulse (Salt & Pepper) Noise:
p(z) = Pa at z=a; Pb at z=b; 0 otherwise
Characterised by random occurrence of completely black (0) or completely white (255) pixels, resembling salt and pepper scattered on the image. Caused by faulty memory cells, bit errors during transmission, or dust on sensor. Best removed by median filter.

Gaussian vs. Impulse Noise — Differences:
• Gaussian: continuous bell-shaped PDF; affects every pixel with small perturbations; all intensities affected; removed by mean filters
• Impulse: PDF has only two spikes at extremes; only a fraction of pixels affected; affected pixels take extreme values (0 or 255); removed by median filter
• Visually: Gaussian → image looks grainy/blurry; Impulse → image has scattered black/white dots

Spatial Noise Filters

🔥 85% · Part B/C

Overview

Spatial filters are applied to the degraded image g(x,y) to estimate the original f(x,y). Three categories:

  • Mean Filters: Linear filters — compute some kind of average in neighborhood. Good for Gaussian noise.
  • Order-Statistics Filters: Non-linear — rank pixels in neighborhood, then select. Good for Salt & Pepper.
  • Adaptive Filters: Vary their behavior based on local statistics. Best overall but more complex.

⭐⭐ Mean Filters (Linear)

1. Arithmetic Mean Filter (Most Basic)

Arithmetic Mean Filter
f̂(x,y) = (1/mn) · Σ Σ g(s,t)
Sum of all mn pixels in the m×n neighborhood, divided by mn. Output = neighborhood average.
Reduces random variations. Blurs edges as side effect. Best for Gaussian noise.

2. Geometric Mean Filter

Geometric Mean Filter
f̂(x,y) = [Π g(s,t)]^(1/mn)
Product of all mn pixels, raised to power 1/mn. Product = π symbol.
Less blurring than arithmetic mean. Problem: if any pixel = 0, product = 0 (edge case).

3. Harmonic Mean Filter

Harmonic Mean Filter
f̂(x,y) = mn / [Σ Σ (1/g(s,t))]
Works well for salt noise (bright impulses). Fails for pepper noise (zeros in denominator).

4. Contraharmonic Mean Filter ⭐ (Important!)

Contraharmonic Mean Filter
f̂(x,y) = Σ g(s,t)^(Q+1) / Σ g(s,t)^Q
Q = filter order (positive or negative). Q > 0 → eliminates pepper noise. Q < 0 → eliminates salt noise. Q = 0 → reduces to arithmetic mean. Q = -1 → reduces to harmonic mean.
🧠
Contraharmonic Trick — Remember with "Salt goes Negative, Pepper goes Positive"

Q > 0: High powers amplify large values → reduces pepper (dark impulses) noise.
Q < 0: High negative powers emphasize small values → reduces salt (bright impulses) noise.
Mnemonic: "S for Salt, S for negative-Sign (Q < 0)" → Salt needs Q negative.

⭐⭐⭐ Order-Statistics Filters (Non-Linear)

1. Median Filter — BEST for Salt & Pepper!

Median Filter
f̂(x,y) = median{g(s,t) | (s,t) ∈ Sxy}
Sort all mn pixel values in neighborhood. Pick the middle value. For mn=9 pixels, pick the 5th value.
📌 Median Filter Numerical Example

3×3 neighborhood values: 5, 8, 3, 100, 6, 4, 7, 9, 200

(100 and 200 are salt/pepper noise)

Sort: 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 100, 200

Median (5th value): 7

Result: 7 (outliers 100 and 200 completely removed!)

Compare with arithmetic mean: (5+8+3+100+6+4+7+9+200)/9 = 342/9 = 38 (corrupted by noise!)

2. Max Filter

f̂(x,y) = max{g(s,t) | (s,t) ∈ Sxy}
Picks the brightest pixel in the neighborhood. Removes pepper noise (dark impulses). Brightens image overall.

3. Min Filter

f̂(x,y) = min{g(s,t) | (s,t) ∈ Sxy}
Picks the darkest pixel. Removes salt noise (bright impulses). Darkens image overall.

4. Midpoint Filter

f̂(x,y) = (1/2)[max{g} + min{g}]
Average of max and min in neighborhood. Good for randomly distributed noise like Gaussian or Uniform.

5. Alpha-Trimmed Mean Filter

Alpha-Trimmed Mean
f̂(x,y) = [1/(mn-d)] · Σ gᵣ(s,t)
Delete d/2 lowest AND d/2 highest values from neighborhood. Average the remaining (mn-d) values.
d=0 → arithmetic mean. d=mn-1 → median filter. Best for mixed Gaussian + S&P noise.
✅
Quick Selector Guide — Which Filter for Which Noise?

Gaussian noise only → Arithmetic mean (or Wiener)
Salt & Pepper only → Median filter
Only pepper (dark spots) → Max filter or Contraharmonic Q>0
Only salt (bright spots) → Min filter or Contraharmonic Q<0
Mixed Gaussian + S&P → Alpha-trimmed mean
General (best quality) → Adaptive Wiener filter

Adaptive Filters

Adaptive filters adjust their behavior based on local image statistics. They perform better than fixed filters because they can preserve edges where they exist and smooth noise in uniform regions.

Adaptive Median Filter

Changes the neighborhood size dynamically. Works on S&P noise. Can preserve detail better than fixed median filter.

Algorithm:

1
Start with small window (e.g., 3×3). Compute z_min, z_max, z_med.
2
If z_min < z_med < z_max: the median is a valid (not noise) pixel. Check if center pixel g(x,y) is noise or signal.
3
If z_min < g(x,y) < z_max → output g(x,y) unchanged (not noise). Else → output z_med (it's noise, replace with median).
4
If z_med is noise (= z_min or z_max) → enlarge window and repeat. Continue until max window size.

Adaptive Local Noise Reduction Filter

Adaptive Local Noise Reduction
f̂(x,y) = g(x,y) - (σ²_η / σ²_L) · [g(x,y) - m_L]
σ²_η = global noise variance. σ²_L = local variance in neighborhood. m_L = local mean.
If local variance >> noise variance → little smoothing (edge area). If local variance ≈ noise variance → heavy smoothing (flat area). Smart!
📝 RTU PYQs — Noise Filters
Q. What is minimum mean square error filtering? (Also: Explain spatial filtering. Define spatial correlation and convolution.)
June 2022 · Part A Q3 · 2 marks | Part B Q2 · 10 marks
Minimum Mean Square Error (MMSE) / Wiener Filtering:

The Wiener filter is an optimal linear filter that minimizes the mean square error (MSE) between the estimated f̂(x,y) and the actual f(x,y):
MSE = E{|f(x,y) - f̂(x,y)|²} = minimum

In frequency domain, the Wiener filter is:
F̂(u,v) = [H*(u,v) / (|H(u,v)|² + Sη(u,v)/Sf(u,v))] · G(u,v)

Where:
• H*(u,v) = complex conjugate of degradation function
• |H(u,v)|² = power spectrum of H
• Sη(u,v) = power spectrum of noise N
• Sf(u,v) = power spectrum of original image f

Simplified form (when ratio is approximately constant K):
F̂(u,v) = [H*(u,v) / (|H(u,v)|² + K)] · G(u,v)
K = Sη/Sf = noise-to-signal power ratio

Key Properties:
• When noise is zero (N=0): reduces to inverse filter F̂ = G/H ✓
• When there is high noise: denominator |H|²+K becomes large → suppresses noise ✓
• K is a tuning parameter — adjusted to trade off between noise removal and blurring

Advantage over Inverse Filter: Wiener filter doesn't amplify noise when H(u,v) is small or zero (where inverse filter divides by ~0 and explodes).

Degradation Functions

80% · Part B/C

What is a Degradation Function?

The degradation function H(u,v) (or PSF — Point Spread Function in spatial domain h(x,y)) describes how the imaging system blurs the original image. Knowing H is essential for restoration.

Three ways to estimate H:

  • Observation: Image a known point source (impulse) and the output IS the PSF h(x,y)
  • Experimentation: Use the same imaging system to capture a test pattern and analyze distortion
  • Mathematical modeling: Derive H from physics of the degradation process

⭐⭐ Atmospheric Turbulence Model

When light passes through the atmosphere, random variations in refractive index cause blurring. Mathematically modeled as:

Atmospheric Turbulence Degradation
H(u,v) = e^(-k(u² + v²)^(5/6))
k = constant related to turbulence severity. Larger k → more blur.
This is a Gaussian-like function in frequency domain → high frequencies are attenuated (blurred).

Three severity levels commonly used:

  • Severe turbulence: k = 0.0025
  • Mild turbulence: k = 0.001
  • Low turbulence: k = 0.00025

⭐⭐⭐ Linear Motion Blur (Most RTU-Asked)

If the image moves horizontally by 'a' pixels during exposure time T, the resulting PSF in frequency domain is:

Linear Horizontal Motion Blur — Frequency Domain
H(u,v) = (T/πu·a) · sin(πu·a) · e^(-jπu·a)
a = amount of motion (pixels), T = exposure time
This creates periodic zeros in H(u,v) — places where H=0 cause problems in inverse filtering!
General Linear Motion (horizontal by a, vertical by b)
H(u,v) = (T/π(ua+vb)) · sin(π(ua+vb)) · e^(-jπ(ua+vb))
Fig 4.1 — Motion blur: image smeared in motion direction; H(u,v) has zeros making inverse filter unstable

Q. Explain the image restoration technique to remove the blur caused by uniform linear motion.
2024 · Part B Q4 · 4 marks
Restoration for Uniform Linear Motion Blur:

Degradation Model:
When a camera or object moves uniformly during exposure, the image is blurred in the direction of motion. For horizontal motion of 'a' pixels during exposure time T:
G(u,v) = H(u,v)·F(u,v) + N(u,v)
Where H(u,v) = (T/πua)·sin(πua)·e^(-jπua)

Problem with Direct Inverse Filtering:
H(u,v) has zeros at u = n/a (for integer n). At these frequencies, G/H = 0/0 → undefined. Also, noise amplification at near-zero H values destroys the result.

Restoration Approach 1 — Truncated Inverse Filter:
F̂(u,v) = G(u,v)/H(u,v) when |H(u,v)| > threshold ε; else F̂ = G(u,v)
This avoids dividing by very small H values. Simple but suboptimal.

Restoration Approach 2 — Wiener Filter (Preferred):
F̂(u,v) = [H*(u,v)/(|H(u,v)|² + K)] · G(u,v)
Where K = Sη/Sf ≈ noise-to-signal ratio (constant approximation)
This avoids division by zero by adding K in denominator. Minimizes MSE.

Steps:
1. Take DFT of blurred image G(u,v) = DFT{g(x,y)}
2. Compute H(u,v) using the motion blur model formula
3. Apply Wiener filter: F̂(u,v) = [H*/(|H|² + K)] · G
4. Take IDFT: f̂(x,y) = IDFT{F̂(u,v)}

Result: Restored image with motion deblurred, with acceptable noise level controlled by K.

Inverse Filtering

75% · Part A/B

Concept of Inverse Filtering

The simplest restoration approach: since G(u,v) = H(u,v)·F(u,v) + N(u,v), just divide both sides by H(u,v):

Inverse Filter
F̂(u,v) = G(u,v) / H(u,v) = F(u,v) + N(u,v)/H(u,v)
Ideally (no noise): F̂ = F + 0 = F → perfect restoration.
With noise: the term N/H can be catastrophic when H(u,v) ≈ 0 → noise is amplified to infinity!
Fig 5.1 — H(u,v) has zeros → 1/H → ∞ → noise amplified to infinity at those frequencies

Problems with Inverse Filtering

  • Zero-crossing problem: When H(u,v) = 0 (zeros of the degradation function), the noise term N/H → ∞. Even tiny noise causes catastrophic artifacts.
  • Near-zero amplification: When H is small but not zero, N/H becomes very large, amplifying noise drastically.
  • No knowledge of noise: Inverse filter completely ignores the noise statistics.
  • Impractical for real images: Real images ALWAYS have some noise, so pure inverse filtering always fails.

Truncated Inverse Filter (Practical Solution)

Truncated Inverse Filter
F̂(u,v) = G(u,v)/H(u,v) if |H(u,v)| > ε (threshold)
F̂(u,v) = 0 if |H(u,v)| ≤ ε
ε is chosen to avoid amplifying noise at near-zero H values. Better than pure inverse filter but still suboptimal.

Wraparound Error in Frequency Domain Filtering

When performing convolution in the frequency domain (via FFT), we must be careful about wraparound error (also called circular convolution error).

Wraparound Error — Why it Happens
Circular convolution (DFT) ≠ Linear convolution (correct result)
The DFT treats the signal as if it were periodic. If the signal is not truly periodic, the "wrap-around" from the end of one period to the start of the next creates spurious artifacts.

Solution: Zero-Padding

1
If image f is M×N and filter h is J×K, the output of linear convolution is (M+J-1)×(N+K-1).
2
Zero-pad both f AND h to size at least (M+J-1)×(N+K-1) before taking FFT.
3
Multiply in frequency domain (now equivalent to linear convolution).
4
Take IFFT and crop to original M×N size.
⚡
Wraparound Error — Exam Answer (2 lines)

Wraparound error occurs when the DFT treats the image as periodic, causing the edges of the image to "wrap around" and corrupt the filtered result. It is prevented by zero-padding both the image and the filter to at least size (M+J-1)×(N+K-1) before computing the DFT.

📝 RTU PYQs — Inverse Filtering & Wraparound
Q. What do you understand by inverse filtering in image processing?
June 2023 · Part A Q6 · 2 marks
Inverse Filtering:

Inverse filtering is the simplest restoration technique. Given the degraded image G(u,v) = H(u,v)·F(u,v) + N(u,v), the inverse filter estimates the original by dividing:
F̂(u,v) = G(u,v)/H(u,v) = F(u,v) + N(u,v)/H(u,v)

Limitation: When H(u,v) ≈ 0, the noise term N/H → ∞, making the restored image meaningless. Pure inverse filtering is impractical for real images that always contain noise.

Improvement: Truncate — only apply 1/H when |H| > threshold ε; zero out at other frequencies.
Q. What is wraparound error in frequency domain filtering?
June 2023 · Part A Q7 · 2 marks
Wraparound Error:

When frequency domain filtering is performed using the DFT, the DFT implicitly assumes the image is periodic (tiles infinitely). When this periodicity assumption is violated, the tail of one period of the image "wraps around" and overlaps with the beginning of the next period, creating corruption in the filtered output. This is called wraparound error or circular convolution artifact.

Prevention: Zero-pad the image and filter to size (M+J-1)×(N+K-1) before computing the DFT, where M×N = image size and J×K = filter size. This ensures that the circular convolution result equals the linear convolution result, eliminating wraparound.

Wiener Filter (MMSE)

🔥 85% · Part A/B/C

Why Wiener Filter?

The inverse filter fails because it ignores noise. The Wiener filter (also called Minimum Mean Square Error filter) solves this by incorporating knowledge of both the degradation H and the noise statistics. It finds the restoration filter that minimizes the statistical expectation of the squared error between the true f and estimated f̂.

Wiener Filter — Optimal Restoration
F̂(u,v) = W(u,v) · G(u,v)
W(u,v) = H*(u,v) / [|H(u,v)|² + Sη(u,v)/Sf(u,v)]
H*(u,v) = complex conjugate of H. Sη = power spectral density of noise. Sf = power spectral density of original image f.
Practical Wiener Filter (constant K approximation)
F̂(u,v) = [H*(u,v) / (|H(u,v)|² + K)] · G(u,v)
K = Sη/Sf ≈ constant = noise-to-signal power spectral ratio. K is the "knob" to tune the filter.
• K = 0 → pure inverse filter (no noise tolerance)
• K → ∞ → output → 0 (too much regularization)
• Optimal K → balanced between noise removal and blur correction

How Wiener Filter Works — Intuition

Fig 6.1 — Wiener filter pipeline: multiply degraded spectrum by W(u,v), then inverse transform

Key Properties of Wiener Filter:

  • When no noise (Sη = 0): W(u,v) = H*(u,v)/|H(u,v)|² = 1/H(u,v) → reduces to inverse filter
  • At low H values: denominator is dominated by K=Sη/Sf → W stays small → noise not amplified
  • At high H values: W ≈ 1/H → acts like inverse filter → good frequency restoration
  • Automatically balances between deblurring and noise suppression based on local SNR

Constrained Least-Squares Filter

65% · Part B

Motivation

Wiener filter requires knowledge of Sη/Sf (noise-to-signal ratio) which is often unknown. The Constrained Least-Squares (CLS) filter provides an alternative that only requires knowledge of the noise mean and variance (easier to estimate).

CLS Filter — Frequency Domain
F̂(u,v) = [H*(u,v) / (|H(u,v)|² + γ|P(u,v)|²)] · G(u,v)
γ = regularization parameter (tuning constant, adjusted iteratively)
P(u,v) = DFT of the Laplacian operator p(x,y) = [[0,-1,0],[-1,4,-1],[0,-1,0]]
The Laplacian P acts as a smoothness constraint — penalizes high-frequency roughness in f̂.

What P(u,v) Does:

The Laplacian in the denominator enforces smoothness in the restored image. This prevents the noisy solution from having wild oscillations. The parameter γ controls the trade-off: larger γ → smoother f̂ (but more blurred). Smaller γ → less smooth (but noise may appear).

💡
CLS vs Wiener — Key Distinction for Exam

Wiener filter: Requires Sη(u,v)/Sf(u,v) — ratio of power spectra (hard to know exactly).
CLS filter: Only requires noise variance σ²η and mean m̄η (much easier to estimate from a uniform region of the image). Iteratively adjusts γ until ||Hf̂ − g||² = ||η||². More practical when noise stats are all that's available.

08

Homomorphic Filtering

🔥 80% · Part B · Repeated!

The Problem: Non-Uniform Illumination

Images are often captured under non-uniform lighting — some parts brighter, some darker. This makes standard enhancement difficult because different regions need different treatment. Homomorphic filtering solves this by separating the illumination (slow, low-frequency) and reflectance (fast, high-frequency) components.

Image Formation Model

Every image f(x,y) can be expressed as a product of two components:

Image Formation Model
f(x,y) = i(x,y) · r(x,y)
i(x,y) = illumination component (0 < i < ∞): overall brightness/lighting — varies slowly (low frequency)
r(x,y) = reflectance component (0 < r < 1): texture, color, surface detail — varies quickly (high frequency)
📌 Illumination vs Reflectance — Real World

Illumination i(x,y): The light falling on a surface. Sunlight from one side makes the left half of a face brighter. Varies smoothly across the image.

Reflectance r(x,y): How much light the surface reflects. A white shirt reflects more than a black shirt. Contains edge and texture details — varies rapidly.

Problem: We want to compress the range of i (reduce bright/dark disparity) while enhancing r (sharpen details). But they're multiplied — can't just subtract!

Homomorphic Filtering Pipeline — MASTER THIS DIAGRAM!

Fig 8.1 — Homomorphic Filtering: ln converts product to sum → DFT → filter → IDFT → exp

Mathematical Derivation — Step by Step

1
Take natural log: ln[f(x,y)] = ln[i(x,y)] + ln[r(x,y)] This converts the product into a SUM — now the two components are additive and can be processed separately.
2
Take DFT: Z(u,v) = DFT{ln[f(x,y)]} = Fi(u,v) + Fr(u,v) Fi = DFT of ln(i) → concentrated at low frequencies. Fr = DFT of ln(r) → spread over high frequencies.
3
Apply Homomorphic Filter H(u,v): S(u,v) = H(u,v)·Z(u,v) The filter has: γL < 1 (attenuates low frequencies = reduces illumination variation) and γH > 1 (boosts high frequencies = enhances reflectance/detail). Typically a modified high-pass filter.
4
Take IDFT: s(x,y) = IDFT{S(u,v)} = s_i(x,y) + s_r(x,y)
5
Take exponential: g(x,y) = e^{s(x,y)} = e^{s_i(x,y)} · e^{s_r(x,y)} = i₀(x,y)·r₀(x,y) Result: compressed illumination range + enhanced reflectance contrast.
Homomorphic Filter Shape H(u,v) — Gaussian Basis
H(u,v) = (γH − γL)[1 − e^{-c(D²(u,v)/D₀²)}] + γL
D(u,v) = distance from center. D₀ = cutoff frequency. c = sharpness control.
At D=0 (center/DC): H ≈ γL (low value → attenuates illumination)
At large D (high freq): H ≈ γH (high value → boosts reflectance)

Effect of Homomorphic Filtering

Frequency RangeComponentFilter ActionVisual Effect
Low (D small)Illumination i(x,y)γL < 1 → attenuateReduces overall brightness variation; brings dark/bright areas closer
High (D large)Reflectance r(x,y)γH > 1 → amplifySharpens edges, enhances texture and detail
💡
Why "Homomorphic"?

The word homomorphic comes from mathematics — a "homomorphism" maps between two algebraic structures. Here, we map from multiplication (f = i·r) to addition (ln f = ln i + ln r) — this is the homomorphism. The log→filter→exp pipeline exploits this mathematical mapping to apply linear filtering (in the log domain) to what is actually a nonlinear (multiplicative) degradation.

Remember the homomorphic pipeline as: Log → Go to Frequency (DFT) → Homomorphic filter → IDFT → Exponential
Or just: ln → DFT → H(u,v) → IDFT → e^x

Q. What is the need of homomorphic filter in image processing? Explain the steps used in homomorphic filtering with a suitable example.
June 2023 · Part B Q4 · 4 marks ⭐ REPEATED!
Need for Homomorphic Filter:

Images often suffer from non-uniform illumination — bright near a window, dark in corners. Standard filtering cannot fix this because illumination i(x,y) and reflectance r(x,y) are multiplied together: f = i·r. We cannot directly apply a filter to separate them.

Homomorphic filtering solves this by exploiting the log transformation to convert the multiplicative model into an additive one, where linear filtering CAN be applied separately.

Steps of Homomorphic Filtering:

Given f(x,y) = i(x,y)·r(x,y) where:
• i(x,y) = illumination (low freq): 0 < i < ∞
• r(x,y) = reflectance (high freq): 0 < r < 1

Step 1: Apply natural log:
ln[f(x,y)] = ln[i(x,y)] + ln[r(x,y)]
Let z(x,y) = ln[f(x,y)]

Step 2: Apply DFT:
Z(u,v) = DFT{z(x,y)} = Fi(u,v) + Fr(u,v)

Step 3: Apply Homomorphic Filter H(u,v):
S(u,v) = H(u,v)·Z(u,v)
H has γL < 1 (attenuates low freq — reduces illumination range)
H has γH > 1 (boosts high freq — enhances reflectance/sharpness)

Step 4: Apply IDFT:
s(x,y) = IDFT{S(u,v)}

Step 5: Apply exponential:
g(x,y) = e^{s(x,y)} = i₀(x,y)·r₀(x,y)

Result: g(x,y) has compressed dynamic range (illumination equalized) and enhanced contrast (reflectance sharpened). Dark areas become visible without blowing out bright areas.

Example: An X-ray image where bone is very bright and soft tissue is very dark. Homomorphic filtering can bring both into a more balanced visible range simultaneously.

Separation of Illumination and Reflectance by Homomorphic Filtering:

Image Formation Model:
f(x,y) = i(x,y) · r(x,y)
i(x,y) = illumination: determined by light source, varies slowly → dominates LOW frequencies
r(x,y) = reflectance: determined by surface, varies quickly at edges → dominates HIGH frequencies

The Problem: Since they are multiplied (not added), we cannot directly filter them separately.

ln[f(x,y)] = ln[i(x,y)] + ln[r(x,y)]
Now they are ADDITIVE! The Fourier transform of ln(f) separates into:
• Low-frequency components ≈ DFT{ln(i)} — illumination
• High-frequency components ≈ DFT{ln(r)} — reflectance

Filtering:
Apply H(u,v) with γL < 1 (suppress low freq = reduce illumination variation) and γH > 1 (boost high freq = enhance reflectance/detail).

After IDFT and exp(), the result g(x,y) = i₀(x,y)·r₀(x,y) has:
• Compressed illumination range (uniform lighting appearance)
• Enhanced reflectance (sharper, more detailed texture and edges)
